# A Complete History of France

This file gives a broad, chronological history of France from prehistory to the present. No single Markdown document can record every village, battle, law, artist, famine, strike, treaty, colony, and election in French history, but this overview is designed to be complete in structure: it follows the major periods, turning points, institutions, social changes, and cultural developments that shaped France.

## Before France: Prehistory and Ancient Gaul

The territory now called France has been inhabited for hundreds of thousands of years. Paleolithic communities left stone tools, burial traces, and some of the most famous prehistoric art in the world, including the cave paintings at Lascaux in the Dordogne and Chauvet in the Ardeche. These works reveal societies with complex symbolic life long before writing.

During the Neolithic period, farming, animal domestication, pottery, polished stone tools, and settled villages spread across western Europe. Megalithic monuments such as the alignments at Carnac in Brittany show that prehistoric communities in the region could organize large labor projects and maintain ritual landscapes.

By the first millennium BCE, much of the region was inhabited by Celtic-speaking peoples whom Greek and Roman writers called Gauls. Gaul was not a unified country. It was a mosaic of tribes and confederations, including the Arverni, Aedui, Sequani, Belgae, Parisii, and many others. These societies practiced agriculture, metalworking, long-distance trade, and warrior aristocratic politics. Greek settlers founded Massalia, modern Marseille, around 600 BCE, creating a Mediterranean trading link between southern Gaul and the wider classical world.

## Roman Gaul

Roman influence in southern Gaul began before Julius Caesar. Rome established the province of Gallia Narbonensis in the late second century BCE, partly to secure routes between Italy and Spain. The decisive transformation came with Caesar's Gallic Wars from 58 to 50 BCE. Caesar defeated multiple Gallic coalitions, most famously the uprising led by Vercingetorix, who surrendered after the siege of Alesia in 52 BCE.

Under Roman rule, Gaul became one of the wealthiest regions of the empire. Cities such as Lugdunum, modern Lyon, became administrative and commercial centers. Roman roads, aqueducts, amphitheaters, villas, baths, and temples reshaped the landscape. Latin spread alongside local languages and eventually became the basis for the Romance dialects from which French, Occitan, Franco-Provencal, and related languages developed.

Christianity reached Gaul during the Roman period. Communities appeared in cities such as Lyon by the second century CE. Christianization was gradual and uneven, but by late antiquity bishops had become important urban leaders.

From the third century onward, the Roman Empire faced political instability, civil wars, inflation, military pressure, and shifting frontier populations. Germanic groups including Franks, Burgundians, Visigoths, and Alamanni entered or settled near Roman territory. By the fifth century, imperial authority in the west weakened severely. In 476 CE the last western Roman emperor was deposed, but Roman institutions, law, Christianity, and urban life continued to influence post-Roman Gaul.

## The Franks and the Birth of Francia

The Franks were a confederation of Germanic peoples who settled in northern Gaul. Their most important early ruler was Clovis I, king of the Salian Franks, who reigned from 481 to 511. Clovis defeated rival Frankish leaders, expanded into Roman Gaul, defeated the Visigoths at Vouille in 507, and converted to Nicene Christianity. His baptism mattered because it aligned the Frankish monarchy with the Gallo-Roman Catholic episcopate rather than with Arian Christianity, which many other Germanic elites followed.

Clovis founded the Merovingian dynasty. After his death, the kingdom was divided among his sons according to Frankish inheritance customs. The resulting political map shifted constantly, with recurring divisions and reunifications among Austrasia, Neustria, Burgundy, and Aquitaine. Merovingian kings ruled through aristocratic networks, bishops, monasteries, and local magnates.

Over time, real power often shifted from kings to major palace officials known as mayors of the palace. The most successful family of mayors became the Carolingians. Charles Martel defeated an Umayyad-led force near Tours or Poitiers in 732, a battle later remembered as a symbolic defense of Christian Europe, though its historical meaning is more complex than later myth suggested.

In 751, Charles Martel's son Pepin the Short deposed the last Merovingian king and became king of the Franks with papal support. The alliance between the Frankish monarchy and the papacy became a central feature of medieval western Europe.

## Carolingians, Vikings, and the Formation of West Francia

Pepin's son Charlemagne built the largest western European empire since Rome. He ruled from 768 to 814 and was crowned emperor by Pope Leo III in Rome on Christmas Day 800. His empire covered much of present-day France, Germany, Belgium, the Netherlands, Switzerland, northern Italy, and parts of central Europe. Charlemagne promoted conquest, Christianization, administrative reform, learning, manuscript copying, and royal oversight through officials known as missi dominici.

After Charlemagne, the empire proved difficult to hold together. His son Louis the Pious inherited it, but conflict among Louis's sons led to civil war. The Treaty of Verdun in 843 divided the Carolingian Empire into three main parts: West Francia, Middle Francia, and East Francia. West Francia is often treated as an ancestor of the kingdom of France, though it was not yet France in the modern sense.

The ninth and tenth centuries saw repeated Viking raids and settlements. In 911, the Frankish king Charles the Simple granted land around the lower Seine to the Viking leader Rollo. This became Normandy. The Normans adopted Christianity, French dialects, and feudal politics while remaining militarily dynamic. In 1066, Duke William of Normandy conquered England, linking French and English aristocratic politics for centuries.

As Carolingian authority declined, local lords, castellans, dukes, counts, bishops, and abbots gained power. The last Carolingian king of West Francia was replaced in 987 by Hugh Capet, count of Paris and duke of the Franks. His election began the Capetian dynasty.

## Capetian France and the High Middle Ages

The early Capetian kings had limited direct control, mostly around the Ile-de-France. Much of the kingdom was ruled by powerful princes: the dukes of Normandy, Aquitaine, Burgundy, Brittany, and others. The Capetians' great achievement was to turn a fragile kingship into an increasingly centralized monarchy.

Several factors helped them. Capetian kings ensured dynastic continuity by associating sons with the throne. They cultivated religious legitimacy, used royal justice, expanded administration, and exploited the weakness or extinction of rival lineages. The monarchy also benefited from the growth of towns, trade, coinage, written records, and legal institutions.

The twelfth and thirteenth centuries were an age of economic and demographic expansion. Forests were cleared, villages multiplied, agriculture intensified, and fairs such as those in Champagne connected northern Europe with the Mediterranean. Gothic architecture emerged in the Ile-de-France and spread across Europe. Cathedrals at Saint-Denis, Chartres, Reims, Amiens, Paris, and elsewhere expressed new engineering, theology, and urban pride.

The University of Paris became one of medieval Europe's leading centers of learning. Scholastic thinkers debated theology, law, philosophy, and Aristotle. Monastic and mendicant orders, including the Cistercians, Franciscans, and Dominicans, shaped religious life.

The French monarchy grew dramatically under Philip II Augustus, who reigned from 1180 to 1223. He defeated King John of England and his allies at Bouvines in 1214 and seized much of the Angevin empire in France, including Normandy. Louis IX, later Saint Louis, reigned from 1226 to 1270 and became a model of Christian kingship, royal justice, crusading zeal, and administrative reform. Philip IV, called Philip the Fair, expanded royal bureaucracy, taxed more aggressively, clashed with the papacy, and convened the Estates-General in 1302.

Medieval France was also marked by exclusion and violence. Jews faced expulsions, confiscations, and persecution. Heresy was suppressed, most famously in the Albigensian Crusade against Catharism in Languedoc in the early thirteenth century. The crusade helped bring southern France more firmly under northern royal influence.

## Crisis, War, and Recovery in the Late Middle Ages

The fourteenth century brought severe crisis. Climate instability, harvest failures, and famine weakened society. The Black Death reached France in 1348 and killed a large portion of the population. Recurrent plague returned for generations. Labor shortages, social unrest, and economic disruption transformed rural and urban life.

The Hundred Years' War, fought intermittently from 1337 to 1453, began from dynastic disputes and deeper political tensions between the kings of France and England. English kings held lands in France and claimed the French crown after the direct Capetian line ended in 1328. Major English victories at Crecy in 1346, Poitiers in 1356, and Agincourt in 1415 exposed weaknesses in French military organization and aristocratic warfare.

France also suffered internal conflicts. The Jacquerie peasant revolt erupted in 1358. Parisian unrest challenged royal authority. Later, civil war between Burgundian and Armagnac factions fractured the kingdom. The Treaty of Troyes in 1420 disinherited the Dauphin Charles and recognized Henry V of England and his heirs as successors to the French throne.

The turning point came with Joan of Arc. In 1429 she helped inspire the relief of Orleans and escorted Charles VII to his coronation at Reims. Captured by Burgundian forces, she was handed to the English, tried for heresy, and executed in Rouen in 1431. She later became a symbol of French resistance, sanctity, and national memory.

Charles VII rebuilt royal authority. Military reforms created more permanent forces, artillery became increasingly important, and royal taxation became more regular. By 1453, the English had lost almost all their continental territories except Calais. The war helped strengthen the monarchy and contributed to a more distinct French political identity.

## Renaissance France and the Wars of Religion

The late fifteenth and sixteenth centuries brought Renaissance culture, royal ambition, and religious fracture. Kings such as Charles VIII, Louis XII, and Francis I launched campaigns in Italy, exposing French elites to Italian art, architecture, politics, and humanism. Francis I promoted royal magnificence, patronized artists and writers, invited Leonardo da Vinci to France, and built or transformed chateaux such as Chambord and Fontainebleau.

The monarchy expanded administrative control. The Ordinance of Villers-Cotterets in 1539 required legal documents in French rather than Latin in many contexts, helping the French language become an instrument of state. Printing, humanism, and reformist religious ideas spread quickly.

Protestantism gained followers in France, especially in its Calvinist form. French Protestants became known as Huguenots. Religious division overlapped with noble factionalism, urban politics, regional tensions, and international rivalry. The French Wars of Religion began in 1562 and lasted, with interruptions, until 1598.

The worst episode was the St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre in August 1572, when thousands of Huguenots were killed in Paris and elsewhere. The monarchy struggled to control violence among Catholic and Protestant factions. The Valois dynasty ended with Henry III's assassination in 1589.

Henry of Navarre, a Protestant Bourbon claimant, became Henry IV. To secure the throne, he converted to Catholicism. In 1598 he issued the Edict of Nantes, granting limited toleration and political protections to Huguenots. His reign restored order, strengthened royal authority, and supported economic recovery until he was assassinated in 1610.

## The Bourbon Monarchy and Absolutism

The seventeenth century saw the consolidation of the Bourbon monarchy. Louis XIII and his chief minister Cardinal Richelieu worked to strengthen the state, reduce the military independence of great nobles and Huguenot strongholds, and position France against Habsburg power. France entered the Thirty Years' War on the anti-Habsburg side despite being a Catholic monarchy.

After Louis XIII's death, Louis XIV became king as a child. During his minority, France experienced the Fronde, a series of revolts by nobles, magistrates, and urban groups between 1648 and 1653. The trauma of the Fronde shaped Louis XIV's later commitment to royal authority.

Louis XIV, who ruled personally from 1661 to 1715, became the emblem of absolutist monarchy. He expanded the royal administration, patronized arts and sciences, and made Versailles the center of court life and political theater. Jean-Baptiste Colbert promoted mercantilist policies, manufacturing, naval development, and colonial commerce. French culture, language, fashion, architecture, and diplomacy gained enormous prestige.

Louis XIV also pursued costly wars: the War of Devolution, the Dutch War, the War of the League of Augsburg, and the War of the Spanish Succession. These conflicts expanded or secured some borders but strained finances and society. In 1685, Louis revoked the Edict of Nantes through the Edict of Fontainebleau, leading to persecution of Protestants and the emigration of many Huguenots.

France's colonial presence expanded in North America, the Caribbean, West Africa, and the Indian Ocean. Colonial commerce was tied to slavery, plantation economies, and Atlantic trade. Saint-Domingue, in present-day Haiti, became one of the world's richest plantation colonies, built on the brutal exploitation of enslaved Africans.

## The Enlightenment and the Crisis of the Old Regime

The eighteenth century combined cultural brilliance with institutional strain. France remained a great power, but repeated wars, court expenditure, tax inequalities, and debt weakened the monarchy. The nobility and clergy enjoyed privileges, while peasants, urban workers, and the bourgeoisie carried many fiscal burdens. The kingdom was a patchwork of regional laws, privileges, weights, measures, and jurisdictions.

The Enlightenment flourished through salons, books, pamphlets, academies, and correspondence. Thinkers such as Montesquieu, Voltaire, Diderot, Rousseau, Condorcet, and many others debated authority, religious tolerance, political liberty, inequality, science, education, and reform. The Encyclopedie attempted to organize and disseminate knowledge in a new critical spirit.

France lost much of its first colonial empire to Britain after the Seven Years' War, which ended in 1763. French support for the American Revolution helped defeat Britain but worsened French state debt. Reforming ministers tried to change taxation and administration, but privileged bodies resisted. By the late 1780s, harvest failures, bread prices, fiscal crisis, and political deadlock forced Louis XVI to summon the Estates-General for 1789, the first meeting since 1614.

## The French Revolution

The French Revolution began in 1789 and transformed France, Europe, and world politics. The Estates-General opened in May 1789. Disputes over voting and representation led the Third Estate to declare itself the National Assembly. In the Tennis Court Oath, deputies vowed not to disperse until France had a constitution. On 14 July 1789, Parisians stormed the Bastille, a royal fortress and prison, which became the Revolution's iconic opening act.

In August 1789, the National Assembly abolished feudal privileges and issued the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen. The declaration proclaimed liberty, equality before the law, national sovereignty, and rights to property, security, and resistance to oppression. The Revolution reorganized France into departments, nationalized church property, reformed the legal system, and attempted to subordinate the Catholic Church to the state through the Civil Constitution of the Clergy.

The Revolution radicalized under pressure from war, counterrevolution, economic crisis, and popular mobilization. The monarchy was overthrown on 10 August 1792. The First Republic was proclaimed in September 1792. Louis XVI was executed in January 1793, and Marie Antoinette in October. France fought coalitions of European monarchies while also facing civil war, notably in the Vendee.

The Jacobin-led Committee of Public Safety, associated with Maximilien Robespierre, directed emergency government during the Reign of Terror in 1793-1794. Revolutionary tribunals, executions, dechristianization campaigns, price controls, mass conscription, and political purges marked this period. The levee en masse mobilized society for war and helped create a new model of national army.

In 1794, the National Convention abolished slavery in French colonies, though Napoleon later restored it in 1802. The Haitian Revolution, led by enslaved and formerly enslaved people including Toussaint Louverture and later Jean-Jacques Dessalines, challenged French colonial rule and resulted in Haitian independence in 1804.

Robespierre fell in July 1794 during the Thermidorian Reaction. The Directory, established in 1795, was corrupt, unstable, and dependent on the army. One of its most successful generals, Napoleon Bonaparte, seized power in the coup of 18 Brumaire in November 1799.

## Napoleon and the First Empire

Napoleon became First Consul and then Emperor of the French in 1804. He preserved some revolutionary gains while creating an authoritarian state. The Napoleonic Code standardized civil law, protected property rights, affirmed legal equality for men, and reinforced patriarchal authority within the family. Napoleon reformed administration through prefects, centralized education, founded the Bank of France, reconciled with the Catholic Church through the Concordat of 1801, and rewarded service through the Legion of Honor.

Napoleon's military campaigns reshaped Europe. French armies defeated Austria, Prussia, Russia, and other powers in a series of victories, including Austerlitz in 1805, Jena-Auerstedt in 1806, and Wagram in 1809. The empire and its satellite states spread legal and administrative reforms, but also imposed occupation, conscription, censorship, extraction, and dynastic rule.

The Peninsular War in Spain and Portugal drained French strength and inspired guerrilla resistance. Napoleon's invasion of Russia in 1812 ended disastrously. A coalition defeated him in 1814, and he abdicated. He returned during the Hundred Days in 1815 but was defeated at Waterloo. Exiled to Saint Helena, he died in 1821.

Napoleon left a contradictory legacy: revolutionary law and careers open to talent, but also dictatorship, imperial war, censorship, and massive casualties. His memory remained a powerful force in French politics.

## Restoration, Revolution, and the Search for a Regime

After Napoleon, the Bourbon monarchy was restored under Louis XVIII and then Charles X. The Restoration attempted to reconcile monarchy with some revolutionary changes, including the Civil Code and property settlements. Ultra-royalists wanted a stronger return to the old order, while liberals defended constitutional limits and civil liberties.

In 1830, Charles X's reactionary ordinances triggered the July Revolution. He was replaced by Louis-Philippe, the "Citizen King," who ruled the July Monarchy. This regime favored the bourgeoisie, limited suffrage, economic modernization, and relative liberalism, but it excluded much of the population from political power.

Industrialization advanced unevenly. Railways, mining, metallurgy, textiles, banking, and urban growth changed French society, though France remained more rural than Britain for much of the nineteenth century. Workers faced harsh conditions, and socialist, republican, Catholic social, and labor movements developed.

In February 1848, revolution overthrew Louis-Philippe and established the Second Republic. The republic introduced universal male suffrage, abolished slavery in French colonies in 1848 under the influence of abolitionists including Victor Schoelcher, and briefly raised hopes for social democracy. Worker unrest in the June Days was violently suppressed. Louis-Napoleon Bonaparte, nephew of Napoleon I, was elected president in December 1848.

In 1851 Louis-Napoleon staged a coup, and in 1852 he became Emperor Napoleon III, founding the Second Empire. His regime combined authoritarian rule with plebiscites, economic modernization, urban redevelopment, and later limited liberalization. Baron Haussmann transformed Paris with boulevards, parks, sewers, and monumental urban planning. France expanded colonial possessions and intervened abroad, including in Crimea, Italy, Mexico, and elsewhere.

The Second Empire collapsed after defeat by Prussia in the Franco-Prussian War of 1870-1871. Napoleon III was captured at Sedan. The German Empire was proclaimed in the Hall of Mirrors at Versailles in January 1871. France ceded Alsace and part of Lorraine to Germany, paid an indemnity, and endured national humiliation.

## The Third Republic and Modern Mass France

The Third Republic was proclaimed in 1870 and survived until 1940. Its early years were fragile. In 1871, Parisian radicals established the Paris Commune after the war and siege of Paris. The Commune experimented with local democracy, social reforms, and revolutionary symbolism before being crushed by the French army during the Bloody Week.

The republic gradually consolidated. Monarchists remained strong at first, but divisions among them helped republicans prevail. The constitutional laws of 1875 created a parliamentary republic. Republican leaders promoted secular public education, civic nationalism, military service, and symbols such as Marianne, the tricolor, and Bastille Day.

Jules Ferry's school laws in the 1880s made primary education free, secular, and compulsory. The republic fought clerical influence, culminating in the 1905 law separating churches and state. French laicite became a defining republican principle, though its meaning remained contested.

The late nineteenth century saw major cultural and political conflicts. The Dreyfus Affair began in 1894 when Alfred Dreyfus, a Jewish army officer, was wrongly convicted of treason. The affair divided France between defenders of the army and nationalist order, and advocates of justice, republican legality, and civil rights. Emile Zola's public intervention helped make the case a national crisis. Dreyfus was eventually exonerated.

France built a vast second colonial empire in North Africa, West Africa, Equatorial Africa, Madagascar, Indochina, the Pacific, and the Caribbean. Colonial rule involved military conquest, forced labor, racial hierarchy, land seizure, extraction, missionary activity, education policies, and resistance by colonized peoples. Colonialism also shaped French politics, economics, migration, and culture.

In metropolitan France, the Belle Epoque brought scientific advances, mass newspapers, consumer culture, cinema, the Eiffel Tower, impressionism and post-impressionism, new entertainment, and social change. It also brought inequality, labor conflict, anarchist violence, nationalist movements, and anxiety about Germany.

## France in the World Wars

World War I began in 1914 after the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand and a cascade of alliance mobilizations. Germany invaded through Belgium and northern France. The First Battle of the Marne stopped the German advance near Paris. The Western Front then hardened into trench warfare.

France suffered enormously. Much of the fighting on the Western Front occurred on French soil. Verdun in 1916 became a symbol of endurance and sacrifice. The war killed around 1.4 million French soldiers and left millions wounded, widowed, orphaned, or traumatized. Colonial soldiers and workers contributed to the war effort. The home front endured censorship, mobilization, shortages, and grief.

The Treaty of Versailles in 1919 returned Alsace-Lorraine to France and imposed penalties on Germany. France sought security through alliances, reparations, occupation policies, and later the Maginot Line. The interwar years were marked by reconstruction, inflation, political polarization, labor struggles, demographic anxiety, and cultural experimentation. The Popular Front government of 1936, led by Leon Blum, introduced paid vacations, collective bargaining, and labor reforms, while facing fierce opposition and economic difficulties.

World War II began in September 1939. Germany attacked France in May 1940 and rapidly defeated French and British forces. An armistice divided France between German occupation in the north and west and the Vichy regime in the south, led by Marshal Philippe Petain. Vichy promoted authoritarian, nationalist, antisemitic, and collaborationist policies. It participated in the persecution and deportation of Jews and repressed opponents.

Charles de Gaulle, in London, called for continued resistance on 18 June 1940. Free French forces, internal Resistance networks, communists, Gaullists, socialists, Christians, Jews, foreigners, and many others contributed to resistance, intelligence, sabotage, rescue, and liberation. Resistance was diverse and often dangerous; many resisters were imprisoned, tortured, deported, or executed.

Allied landings in Normandy on 6 June 1944 and in Provence in August 1944 helped liberate France. Paris was liberated in August 1944. De Gaulle led the Provisional Government. After liberation, France dealt with collaboration through legal purges and informal reprisals, while also constructing a memory centered for decades on resistance and national restoration.

## The Fourth Republic, Decolonization, and Postwar Reconstruction

Postwar France rebuilt physically, politically, and socially. Women gained the right to vote in 1944 and first voted in national elections in 1945. The Fourth Republic was established in 1946. It created or expanded major social institutions, including social security, nationalized key sectors, and supported economic planning.

The postwar economic boom, later called the Trente Glorieuses, transformed France between roughly 1945 and 1975. Industrial growth, modernization, mass consumption, automobiles, household appliances, public housing, education expansion, and rural decline changed daily life. Immigration from southern Europe, North Africa, sub-Saharan Africa, and elsewhere helped supply labor and reshaped French society.

The Fourth Republic was weakened by unstable parliamentary coalitions and colonial wars. France fought to reassert control in Indochina after World War II but was defeated by Vietnamese forces at Dien Bien Phu in 1954. The Geneva Accords ended French Indochina and divided Vietnam.

Algeria posed an even deeper crisis because it was legally integrated into France and had a large European settler population alongside a disenfranchised Muslim majority. The Algerian War began in 1954 between the French state and the National Liberation Front. The conflict involved guerrilla war, terrorism, torture, repression, forced displacement, political crisis, and violence in both Algeria and metropolitan France.

In May 1958, the Algerian crisis brought down the Fourth Republic. Charles de Gaulle returned to power and oversaw the creation of a new constitution.

## The Fifth Republic

The Fifth Republic began in 1958. Its constitution created a stronger presidency, a more stable executive, and institutions designed to avoid the parliamentary instability of the previous regime. De Gaulle became president in 1959.

De Gaulle eventually accepted Algerian self-determination despite fierce opposition from many settlers and parts of the army. The Evian Accords led to Algerian independence in 1962. The end of the war brought the migration of pieds-noirs, Algerian Jews, and Harkis to France, along with enduring memory conflicts.

De Gaulle pursued an independent foreign policy. France developed nuclear weapons, withdrew from NATO's integrated military command in 1966 while remaining in the alliance, recognized the People's Republic of China, and sought a Europe of states. Franco-German reconciliation became central to European integration, especially after the Elysee Treaty of 1963.

The Fifth Republic also presided over modernization: highways, nuclear energy, aerospace, telecommunications, universities, consumer society, and state-led industrial projects. Yet social tensions persisted.

In May 1968, student protests and worker strikes produced the largest general strike in French history. The movement challenged hierarchy, capitalism, patriarchy, university structures, labor relations, and cultural conservatism. De Gaulle survived politically in the short term but resigned in 1969 after losing a referendum.

Georges Pompidou continued modernization. Valery Giscard d'Estaing, elected in 1974, introduced social reforms including the lowering of the voting age to 18, expansion of divorce reform, and the legalization of abortion through the Veil Law of 1975, championed by Simone Veil.

## France Since the 1970s

The oil shocks of the 1970s ended the long postwar boom. France faced slower growth, deindustrialization, unemployment, inflation, and debates over the welfare state. Immigration became a central political issue, often tied to questions of identity, secularism, racism, integration, policing, housing, and colonial memory.

In 1981, Francois Mitterrand became the first Socialist president of the Fifth Republic. His government abolished the death penalty, nationalized major industries and banks, expanded cultural policy, and introduced social reforms. Economic difficulties led to a turn toward austerity in 1983. Mitterrand's long presidency also saw European integration deepen, including the path toward the Maastricht Treaty and the euro.

French politics developed alternating periods of left and right government, as well as "cohabitation," when the president and parliamentary majority came from opposing camps. Jacques Chirac was president from 1995 to 2007. He acknowledged the French state's responsibility for the wartime roundup and deportation of Jews, opposed the 2003 Iraq War, and faced major social protests over pension and labor reforms.

France adopted the euro as an accounting currency in 1999 and introduced euro notes and coins in 2002. European integration reshaped monetary policy, borders, law, trade, and political debate. France remained a founding power of the European Union while also producing strong currents of Euroskepticism.

The early twenty-first century brought new challenges: terrorism, social inequality, suburban unrest, climate policy, globalization, digital change, and distrust of political elites. The 2005 riots in many suburbs exposed anger over policing, unemployment, discrimination, and exclusion. Terrorist attacks in 2015 and 2016, including the Charlie Hebdo attack, the November 2015 Paris attacks, and the Nice attack, deeply affected national life and intensified debates over security, secularism, Islam, and civil liberties.

Nicolas Sarkozy, Francois Hollande, and Emmanuel Macron each attempted reforms amid social resistance. Macron, first elected in 2017 and reelected in 2022, pursued labor, tax, pension, education, and European reforms while facing major protests, including the Yellow Vest movement beginning in 2018 and widespread demonstrations against pension reform.

France today is a constitutional republic, a nuclear power, a permanent member of the United Nations Security Council, a central member of the European Union, and a country still shaped by its revolutionary tradition, republican ideals, regional cultures, colonial legacies, social conflicts, and global ambitions.

## Long-Term Themes

### State Formation

French history is marked by the long growth of the state. From Capetian royal justice to Bourbon absolutism, from Napoleonic prefects to republican administration, France developed a powerful centralized tradition. Yet this centralization has always coexisted with local identities, regional languages, municipal politics, and resistance to Parisian authority.

### Revolution and Republic

France's modern identity is inseparable from revolution. The Revolution of 1789 redefined sovereignty, citizenship, rights, law, property, and political legitimacy. Later revolutions in 1830, 1848, and 1871, along with the upheavals of 1968, show a recurring pattern: political authority in France has repeatedly been challenged from the street, the workplace, the university, and the barricade.

### Religion and Secularism

Christianity shaped France for more than fifteen centuries, from Roman Gaul through monarchy, parish life, education, art, and social organization. The Reformation and Wars of Religion fractured the kingdom. The Revolution attacked church privilege. The Third Republic institutionalized secular education and laicite. In modern France, secularism remains a living and contested principle.

### Language and Culture

French became a language of administration, literature, diplomacy, and elite culture, but France historically contained many languages: Breton, Basque, Occitan, Alsatian, Corsican, Catalan, Flemish, Franco-Provencal, and others. State schooling and military service spread standard French. French culture has had global influence in philosophy, literature, painting, cinema, fashion, cuisine, architecture, music, and science.

### Empire and Colonialism

France's history is not only European. Its empires in the Americas, Africa, Asia, and the Pacific shaped trade, warfare, migration, race, law, and identity. Slavery, abolition, colonial conquest, assimilationist ideology, forced labor, anti-colonial resistance, and decolonization remain central to understanding modern France.

### Social Conflict

French society has repeatedly been transformed by conflict among classes, estates, professions, regions, generations, and political movements. Peasant revolts, urban uprisings, labor strikes, feminist campaigns, student movements, anti-racist activism, and rural protests all belong to the national story.

### Europe and the World

France has been a European great power for centuries. Its rivalry and reconciliation with Germany shaped the continent. Its partnership with Britain, conflict with Britain, relationship with the United States, role in NATO, and leadership in the European Union have all influenced its place in the world.

- c. 600 BCE: Greek settlers found Massalia, modern Marseille.
- 58-50 BCE: Julius Caesar conquers Gaul.
- 52 BCE: Vercingetorix is defeated at Alesia.
- 2nd-4th centuries CE: Christianity spreads in Roman Gaul.
- 476: Conventional date for the fall of the Western Roman Empire.
- 481-511: Reign of Clovis I, founder of Merovingian power.
- 507: Clovis defeats the Visigoths at Vouille.
- 732: Charles Martel defeats an Umayyad-led force near Tours/Poitiers.
- 751: Pepin the Short becomes king, founding Carolingian royal power.
- 800: Charlemagne is crowned emperor in Rome.
- 843: Treaty of Verdun divides the Carolingian Empire; West Francia emerges.
- 911: Viking settlement in Normandy begins under Rollo.
- 987: Hugh Capet becomes king, beginning the Capetian dynasty.
- 1066: William of Normandy conquers England.
- 1214: Philip II wins the Battle of Bouvines.
- 1226-1270: Reign of Louis IX.
- 1302: Philip IV convenes the Estates-General.
- 1337-1453: Hundred Years' War.
- 1348: Black Death reaches France.
- 1429: Joan of Arc helps relieve Orleans.
- 1431: Joan of Arc is executed at Rouen.
- 1453: End of the Hundred Years' War; English power in France is largely expelled.
- 1539: Ordinance of Villers-Cotterets strengthens the use of French in legal administration.
- 1562-1598: French Wars of Religion.
- 1572: St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre.
- 1598: Henry IV issues the Edict of Nantes.
- 1610: Henry IV is assassinated.
- 1648-1653: The Fronde.
- 1661-1715: Personal rule of Louis XIV.
- 1685: Revocation of the Edict of Nantes.
- 1715: Death of Louis XIV.
- 1756-1763: Seven Years' War; France loses much of its first colonial empire.
- 1789: Estates-General, National Assembly, Bastille, Declaration of Rights.
- 1792: Monarchy abolished; First Republic proclaimed.
- 1793: Louis XVI executed; Reign of Terror intensifies.
- 1794: Slavery abolished in French colonies by the revolutionary government.
- 1799: Napoleon seizes power in the coup of 18 Brumaire.
- 1804: Napoleon becomes emperor; Haitian independence.
- 1815: Napoleon defeated at Waterloo.
- 1830: July Revolution.
- 1848: Second Republic; universal male suffrage; abolition of slavery in French colonies.
- 1852: Napoleon III establishes the Second Empire.
- 1870-1871: Franco-Prussian War; fall of the Second Empire.
- 1871: Paris Commune.
- 1875: Constitutional laws of the Third Republic.
- 1880s: Jules Ferry school laws.
- 1894-1906: Dreyfus Affair.
- 1905: Separation of churches and state.
- 1914-1918: World War I.
- 1916: Battle of Verdun.
- 1936: Popular Front government.
- 1939-1945: World War II.
- 1940: Fall of France; Vichy regime established; de Gaulle's 18 June appeal.
- 1944: Liberation of Paris; women gain voting rights.
- 1946: Fourth Republic established.
- 1954: Dien Bien Phu; beginning of the Algerian War.
- 1958: Fifth Republic founded.
- 1962: Algerian independence.
- 1968: May student and worker revolt.
- 1975: Veil Law legalizes abortion.
- 1981: Francois Mitterrand elected; death penalty abolished.
- 1992: Maastricht Treaty approved by French referendum.
- 2002: Euro notes and coins enter circulation.
- 2005: Suburban riots; EU constitutional treaty rejected by referendum.
- 2015: Major terrorist attacks in Paris.
- 2017: Emmanuel Macron elected president.
- 2018-2019: Yellow Vest movement.
- 2022: Macron reelected president.
- 2023: Major protests against pension reform.

## Final Note

France is not a single uninterrupted story of inevitable nationhood. It is the result of layered histories: prehistoric settlement, Celtic Gaul, Roman rule, Frankish monarchy, feudal lordship, Capetian state-building, religious conflict, absolutism, revolution, empire, republic, colonial expansion, occupation, resistance, decolonization, European integration, and continuing democratic argument. Its history is therefore both national and transnational, metropolitan and colonial, institutional and social, glorious and violent, unified and deeply contested.
